What is a Breadboard?
Prototype boards (commonly called breadboards) are used for wiring circuits together without soldering. They allow you to rapidly reconfigure components so you can prototype circuit designs. If you took the breadboard apart, you would see that each column of holes is connected by a metal strip. If you connect a wire to one of the holes, anything you plug into another hole in the same column would be connected to that wire. Note that the gap down the center of the board electrically divides the columns of holes in the top half of the board from those in bottom half.

Assembly

  1. Headers

    Insert the headers (image below) through the small holes on the front of the PCB. Make sure the connector is flush with the surface of the PCB.

  2. Solder the headers

    While holding the headers in place, flip the board over and solder all the connector pins. Note: It may be helpful to put the breadboard under the PCB to help hold it in place while soldering. What is the point of soldering? DO NOT solder on the front of the board. Soldering Demo

  3. Attach the breadboard

    Flip the board back over to the side that says “front.” Peel off the cover on the back of the breadboard and attach it below the headers as shown in the image below. Attach it as close as possible to the headers to leave room for the stand's screws! What is a breadboard?

  4. Bolt the Bluefruit and the display

    Attach the display (TFT Gizmo) to the front (image bottom/left) and the Bluefruit to the back (image bottom/right) of the circuit board using the 12 provided screws. Start each of the screws in the order highlighted below (image middle/left). Once they are all started, then tighten them down so that standoffs are flush with the front of the board (image middle/right). Note: The TFT Gizmo label on the display and the USB connector on the Bluefruit should be aligned towards the top of the board. You'll have to remove the orange screw covers on the display's standoffs. Be gentle when using the screws to attach the display. It is fragile!

    Once complete, it should look like this:

  5. Stand

    Attach the circuit board to the acrylic stand.

Setup your Adafruit board

  1. Using the USB cable, plug your board into your computer
  2. Rapidly push the “reset” button in the middle of your board two times:

  3. All the LEDs around the outside should turn green and you should have a red and green LED on either side of your USB cable. You should also see CPLAYBOOT show up as a drive in your computer's file explorer.

  4. Click here to download the files you will need.
  5. Open your downloaded folder and the “Circuit Playground Lab Files” folder inside it. If you see the WinRAR icon, you will likely need to uninstall the program to access the files. See the steps in Troubleshooting.
  6. Drag the file named adafruit-circuitpython-circuitplayground_bluefruit-en_US-#.#.# from the Circuit Playground Lab Files folder you just opened onto your CPLAYBOOT drive highlighted in step 3.
  7. After a few seconds, the outer ring of LEDs should start flashing green and the drive name should change to CIRCUITPY. When you click on that drive, you should see the files and folders below.
  8. If you closed it, open the "Circuit Playground Lab Files" folder you downloaded. Then open the "lib files" folder.

    Drag/copy all the documents (shown in the video below) from that folder /into the "lib" folder in your CIRCUITPY drive. Do not copy the “lib files” folder, just the items inside it!

Using Mu

  1. to view the Bluefruit's components. Mouse over the board or the list of components to highlight each part. You can view this any time by clicking the "Bluefruit Board" button in the instructions below.
  2. If you haven't done so already, download and install Mu Editor. Select "CircuitPython" for the "mode".
  3. Open the Mu Editor.
  4. In the Mu editor, turn on the Serial display and the Plotter.
  5. If you don't see a tab in the Mu editor named "code.py", click "Load" and navigate to your CIRCUITPY drive and open the “code” file.
